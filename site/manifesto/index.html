Manifesto

I've spent a decade fitting myself into other people's shapes. Job titles. Role descriptions. Consultancy slots. Each container asked me to file off the parts of myself that didn't match — the parts that crossed boundaries, the parts that cared about the substrate more than the surface, the parts that wanted to build the whole thing rather than hand off a piece of it. The work still came out — the pipelines, the systems, the 60% defect reduction at Vivo Energy — but it came out despite the container, not because of it. Every role rewarded a version of me that was less than the whole.

Orin exists because I'm done disappearing.

This isn't an agency built around a service. It's a vehicle for being legibly and specifically myself, and offering that specificity to businesses that recognise it when they see it. The service list will change as I change. The client list will change as I learn. What won't change is the worldview underneath — because that's the thing being sold. Everything else is downstream.

What I believe

I believe design does not live in isolation. It lives inside a system, and the system lives inside a business, and the business exists to make people's lives better. Take design out of that chain and it becomes decoration. Put it back in and it becomes meaningful. Design for the sake of design is empty to me. Design as the visible edge of a working system that serves real people through a real business — that's the work worth doing.

I believe design and development are the same craft, artificially separated. The industry treats them as two disciplines that hand off to each other, and the handoff is where most value leaks out. Broken pipelines. Out-of-date components. Legacy font stacks nobody wants to touch. Designers fighting the system to ship anything real. Engineers rebuilding what designers already built. All of this is the cost of pretending these are two different jobs. They aren't. They're one continuous act of building, and I want to work in the middle of it, not on one side of it.

I believe the system is the main thing. Not the artefact, not the deliverable, not the Figma file, not the sprint output. The system. Individual components matter, but they matter because they fit into something larger. The sum is greater than the parts, and the sum is what a business actually uses. When the substrate is good, everything on top of it gets better. When the substrate is broken, no amount of surface craft compensates.

I believe craft matters, but it isn't the point. The point is solving a business problem so that people can do their work more simply, more efficiently, and with more confidence. The Vivo Energy checkout wasn't slow because the pixels were wrong. It was slow because users didn't trust it, and they didn't trust it because the underlying system was inconsistent. Fixing that wasn't a design problem in the way design is usually described. It was a systems problem that happened to require design skill to solve. That's the kind of work I want to do.

I believe good work happens incrementally. Ship the MVP even if it's slightly off. Learn from it. Improve it. Ship again. Design systems, websites, businesses, agencies — none of them arrive fully formed. They emerge through iteration, and the willingness to iterate is more valuable than the ability to plan.

What Orin does

Orin builds things. Not deliverables. Working things — token pipelines, design systems, websites, checkout flows, the substrate that businesses run on. The engagement starts with understanding the whole system end-to-end, because I can't operate without the full picture. Then it moves into building — usually in the space between design and engineering, often crossing into infrastructure that most consultancies won't touch. Figma. Style Dictionary. GitHub. Cloudflare. Whatever the working thing requires.

The work is iterative. Orin doesn't drop a big deliverable and leave. Orin builds the first version, watches how it behaves, improves it, and stays close enough to keep improving it as the business grows. The relationship is the delivery vehicle. Without the relationship, the iteration can't happen, and without the iteration, the system decays back into an artefact.

The measure of the work is what happens after handoff, not at handoff. Defect rates. Component reuse. Time-to-ship. User trust. Business outcomes. These are the numbers I want on my case studies, because these are the numbers the work is actually for.

Who Orin is for

Orin is for founders, product leaders, and engineering directors who've been burned by design that looked good and shipped badly — and who can tell the difference within ten minutes of a conversation. It's for businesses that already know their substrate is broken and want someone who will actually go and fix it, not write a strategy deck about it. It's for people who value the relationship and share the belief that the system is the main thing.

Orin is not for clients who want a design system in forty hours because the budget said so. That request treats design as a commodity, and it isn't one. Orin is not for clients who want brand guidelines, marketing surfaces, or social media assets — those are legitimate needs, but they aren't what I do, and pretending otherwise would waste everyone's time. Orin is not for clients who want a vendor. Orin is a partner or nothing.

How the work happens

The work is direct. Small engagements to start, so both sides can learn how the other operates. Then longer ones, iteratively scoped, priced against outcomes rather than hours where possible. Communication is honest — I'd rather tell a client the hard truth about their system than sell them polish they don't need. I write, I build, I ship. I don't produce decks about work I could have already done in the time.

The offer will evolve because I will. What won't evolve is the belief that design is a means, systems are the mechanism, and business is the vehicle for making people's lives better. That's the fixed point. Everything else adapts around it.

Orin is what happens when I stop trying to fit and start building the shape I actually am.

Warren G Rossiter — 1 July 2026
